Compelling Communication

Most recently, I was Founder & CEO of Impart.

Working with little direction and high ambiguity, I found introspection invaluable to thinking clearly and making decisions. Specifically, I would articulate my thoughts in writing and share these with trusted teammates, advisers, and friends.

in⋅tro⋅spec⋅tion: the examination or observation of one's own mental and emotional processes.

Logic and emotions are both part of decision-making and essential to daring greatly. They can be powerful tools in entrepreneurship, where there are no right answers. Taking time to communicate your thoughts can provide conviction down the road, when others (and future you) question you - par for the course.

Plus introspection and communication are free resources. Entrepreneurs like free!

Values

Once I recruited a stellar team, I knew we could build the best Impart by leveraging each person's unique perspective. This meant trusting individuals and giving them as much autonomy as possible.

To ensure autonomy leads individuals to the best decisions for the company, I emphasized Impart's vision (above) and values (below). Looking back, I believe values are more important than vision. As a startup learns and grows, their vision will likely change (pivot!), while values will become more ingrained (founders inculcate others).

For best practices, I looked to Programming Your Culture, a chapter from The Hard Thing about Hard Things. Author and entrepreneur Ben Horowitz states, company culture should:
- Distinguish you from competitors
- Ensure that critical operating values persist such as delighting customers or making beautiful products
- Help you identify employees that fit with your mission

Inspired by the idea that it's as important to know who you are as it is to know who you're not, I articulated Impart's values:

  • Humans over Technology
  • Relationships over Transactions
  • Mindfulness over Expedience
  • Balance over Perfection

Failure (a.k.a. Quitting)

I believe failure is synonymous with quitting. When you fail, there's probably some torturous way to keep going. But you decided it's not worth it and quit. That's why it feels so bad - quitting is a choice.

Now that I've posited failure is quitting, it's also true - failure is success in the form of learning. Fortunately there's enough people posting successory quotes about failure that it's gained respect.

On the other hand, quitting often gets an overly bad rep. While a high amount of stick-with-it-ness is important, I believe everyone has their limits. If we think of quitting like failure - a form of learning - it becomes a more understandable choice. Are you optimally balancing learning, opportunity, risk, and opportunity costs? Hint: There's no right answer :)
